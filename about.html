A short sprinting story

Sprinting is one of the oldest competitive sports. The first recorded 100‑meter world record dates back to the 1930s, when Jesse Owens ran 10.3 seconds in Berlin in 1936.

Over decades, athletes slowly shaved fractions of a second off that time through better training, track surfaces, and footwear.

Sprinting also became a global stage for cultural moments—from Owens’ triumph over racism in 1936 to the modern global fascination with Olympic speed.

Usain Bolt and the 9.58 world record

Jamaican sprinter Usain Bolt is widely regarded as the greatest sprinter of all time.

In 2009, at the World Championships in Berlin, he ran the 100 meters in 9.58 seconds, setting the current world record that still stands today.

Bolt also holds the 200‑meter world record (19.19 seconds) and helped Jamaica set the 4×100‑meter world record (36.84 seconds).

His success came from a mix of height, stride length, raw power, and a visibly relaxed running style that made his speed look almost effortless.

Bolt’s dominance continued across three Olympic Games (2008, 2012, 2016), where he claimed three straight golds in both the 100 and 200 meters.

For many fans, Bolt turned sprinting into a spectacle—fast, fun, and larger than life.

What this site covers

The sprinting section explains basic sprinting concepts, types of races, and training ideas at a beginner level.

The contact page lets you get in touch with questions about sprinting, the site itself, or general athletics topics.

This is not a professional coaching resource, but a college project aimed at practicing web design while keeping the theme grounded in real‑world athletics.
